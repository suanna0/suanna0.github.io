KLEE

Writer, Director, Editor, Colorist, 2025.

“One of the persistent images of San Francisco Bay Area Architecture [is] the natural house taking its place unpretentiously in the environment…the 'immensity of the scene,' militiated against the house as a highly polished object set against a backdrop.” –Woodbridge Sally B. Bay Area Houses (1976)

In my critical studies class, we were asked to write about a monument. My mind wandered to estate-turned museums in secluded wilderness: Frank Lloyd Wright's Fallingwater, Hearst Castle in San Simeon, and Filoli in Woodside. As a teenager, my dad and I would take long drives down the California Coast, visiting these landmarks. As a result, I began constructing a taste for what the dream home I hoped to live in one-day may look like. This notion of a dream home that works in conjunction with the landscape, that is then transformed into a monument/source of tourism function as a display of wealth, a time capsule into what it was like to be part of the socialite class in that time period.

This fascination with estates-turned-monuments led me to question the role of architecture in realizing our fantasies. Perhaps we can perfect our environment until we can no longer feel pain. KLEE, a fictional interior design service, explores how the home, meant to be a place of comfort, can also serve as a site for superficial performance.

Synopsis:

John's dimly lit bedroom, cluttered with a gamer chair and pizza boxes, sets the stage for an awkward morning after—sending his date, Jane, fleeing in horror. Realizing his living space might need some serious work, John finds himself doom-scrolling Pinterest for inspiration. Enter Sally, a sharp-witted interior designer who provides him with a vision (and a price tag) to bring his home to life.

With a checklist in hand and a package of design ideas, John enlists his friends to help revamp his space, piece by piece. A pickup truck full of furniture and some teamwork later, his once-disastrous apartment is unrecognizable. As the final touch, everyone—including Jane and Sally— celebrates the transformation with an all-out dance party to Got Me Started, proving that sometimes, a new couch can change everything.
